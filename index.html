Do Vision-Language Models Understand CultureMix?

What is Culture Mix?

Culture mix refers to scenes where cultural cues like food, attire, or architecture from different cultures appear together. (E.g., global potluck, enjoying food from your own culture while traveling abroad)

This project page brings together three benchmarks showing how vision-language models drift, overfit, and misclassify when food, people, landmarks, flags, and cultural artifacts are mixed inside one scene.

Why is this important?

Culture mixing reveals model failures that evaluations in a single cultural context may miss

01

Dominant cue bias

Models often anchor on the most visually salient cue, such as a person’s appearance or a famous landmark, and overwrite the cultural identity of the focal item.

02

Context sensitivity

The same food can receive different predictions depending on who is eating it or the environment in which it appears, even when the food itself remains unchanged.

03

Resource asymmetry

High-resource cultures are typically more robust to perturbation, while low-resource cultures experience larger drops in accuracy and larger sensitivity to contextual change.

Our three papers ask one shared question

When cultural cues conflict in a single scene, which cues do models rely on most?

Each paper isolates a different failure mode and reveals where models collapse under culturally mixed context.
