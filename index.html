Three papers, one shared question

Each paper probes a different failure mode: food mixed with people, food mixed with background, or cultural attributes mixed across geography and identity.

Why It Matters

Culture mixing exposes shortcuts that single-culture benchmarks can miss

Dominant cue bias

Models often anchor on the most visually salient cue, such as a person’s appearance or a famous landmark, and overwrite the cultural identity of the focal item.

Context sensitivity

The same food can receive different predictions depending on who is eating it or which environment appears behind it, even when the object itself is unchanged.

Resource asymmetry

High-resource cultures are typically more robust to perturbation, while low-resource cultures experience larger drops in accuracy and larger sensitivity to contextual change.
